# Why I Have a Very Small Phone
*Simon Whittome - June 2026*

Every time I order a hot drink from a coffee shop, the man or woman behind the counter often plucks up the courage to start a conversation with me - and how could I blame them! Maybe its my charm, my charismatic approach, my well groomed facial hair. Unfortunately, I'm pretty sure its none of the above.

Its most likely because I have a very small phone. 

It isn't just a small iPhone either - I have a phone with a screen smaller than a pack of cards, and about the same size as a pack of Tic-Tacs. I have a Jelly Star, and it comes with the ability to tease a curious smile and conversation out of most serving staff. This peculiar ability doesn't stop at baristas.

![[jellystar.png]]

If I had a small potato for every time I'd been in a Wetherspoons and someone saw my phone and stopped their conversation just to ask me 'what is that? Is that your phone?', then I'd have enough potatoes to make a lovely potato salad. When other people see my phone, the most common reactions are,

> "That's so cool! Where did you get it?"
>
>"What is that? Can it take photos?"

Closer friends go on to ask me
>"Why did you get that phone?"

This page should hopefully give something of an answer to that question, along with what I've done to achieve it, and a review of anything I might do differently when buying my next phone when my small Chinese phone eventually blows up (hopefully metaphorically). First, I'll go through the circumstances that led me to switch from an iPhone 12 to the Jelly Star, then I'll talk about how I've set it up to be the best phone it can be.

### "Why did you get that phone?"

For my undergraduate degree, I did Tonmeister at Surrey, and I absolutely loved it. Its a four year program where the third year is a placement year, and I did my placement at a startup called Setmixer. When I say I 'did my placement at', I don't mean geographically at Setmixer because there was no central office, it was an entirely remote company. From June 2024 to June 2025, I worked for Setmixer as a fully remote DevOps team member, and I had great opportunities that taught me loads about Git version control, AWS virtual environments, and signal processing constrained by hardware and time.

For all of its positives, the medium of remote work was, and still is, something I struggle with. For 5 days a week over the summer months, from 9 to 5 I logged in and did my work, which had moments of interest, but was mostly monotonous data labelling. I had 2 meetings a week. I lived at home. I got out every Sunday to go to church. I played video games. I watched YouTube. I scrolled plenty of Instagram Reels.

The idiom 'a ticking time bomb' refers to a situation or person who, if things carry on as they are, will eventually blow up into a something nasty. YouTube, Instagram, few real life social encounters, and staring at a screen all day in my home-office-bedroom for work; These were all contributors to my homemade ticking time bomb of feeling pretty down a lot of the time.

Subconsciously, I began feeling dreary and upset with the world. Not in the 'I hate the world! Its awful' sense, but rather in the quiet discontent caused by hoping for something, but getting another. Through Instagram and YouTube, I hoped to receive the social engagement I wasn't getting in real life, but I was getting something else. I saw everyone else having a great time as a stand in for having a great time myself.

 I was using my phone as a way of engaging with the world, but not getting anything back from my engagement. Social media became a stimulant, a drug, a replacement for God given community, and I hadn't even noticed the swap. It just happened to me.

Around October 2024, I had had enough. I had begun to diagnose the issue as a lack of real social connection, which was caused by a lack of a real working environment, a lack of local friends in my hometown, and I had just noticed that I was using my phone as an inadequate replacement for these things.

You might be thinking "The answer to not having enough real social interaction is to *get out more*, to meet new people and to *make new friends*". And you'd be right!

But there's something else. I wasn't just using my phone as a replacement for social interactions. I was addicted to it. I was spending about 5 hours a day using my phone, which is statistically in line with a majority of people in my generation ([source](https://www.bbc.co.uk/news/articles/crrqdw9v02e)). Maybe addiction isn't the right word, but its the word I'm going to use because even after setting out to put my phone down for a day, I could feel the tug of wanting to get it back out again to check if anyone had called, or more honestly just to doomscroll again.

 Once I saw that when I gave so much time to my phone, I was giving my time to something which returned something far less valuable, I decided that I should delete the apps. Surely if I just take the ability to use Instagram and YouTube off of my phone, I'd stop using them? But I quickly found this wasn't enough as I'd just re-download them again. As a very strong willed person, I found my lack of willpower very disturbing.

This is when I decided to investigate getting a 'dumbphone'.

### Finding a dumbphone

When I went to school in year 10, I had quite the novel flip phone. It had snake, and I modded it to play java games I downloaded online so I could parkour around a little digital city (it was unbelievably fun). These kind of phones, also called feature phones, are now affectionately called 'dumbphones' by an increasingly growing online community of 'digital minimalists'.

A dumbphone is a phone just for calling, texting, and maybe a bit of snake. Its sole purpose is a stripped down experience - the teleological phone experience, pure and concentrated in its purpose. The term has garnered much popularity online, where a [subreddit](https://www.reddit.com/r/dumbphones/) now exists - users ask for advice and show off their 'daily carry'

>[!faq]
>Users posting on this subreddit have often gotten very deep into the 'intentional technology use' lifestyle, and have divided their phone functionality into several objects. Instead of one single phone that does everything, a user might carry a digital camera, mp3 player, emulator console, e-reader, notebook, headphones. Pretty cool,  but that's also a lot of stuff to carry around. I've opted very much for the phone, wallet keys approach.

In 2024, my objectives in buying a new phone were pretty clear: I wanted to be less addicted, and more social. I wanted to not even be tempted to 'doom-scroll', and still be able to coordinate social activities using a phone.

I started by looking into feature phones - some of which are really fun and nostalgic. Unfortunately, they lacked features I really grew to love of a smartphone - NFC payments, maps, and critically non addictive social apps like WhatsApp. I also enjoyed being able to take decent photos, and use streaming services, also non addictive for me. These are smartphone features, not feature phone features. I moved on from feature phones, and began looking into smart-dumbphones (which begins to become a bit of an oxymoron).

Here are the phones that I looked into:

**Cat S22 flip**
Cool flip phone design, runs Android Go (a stripped down version of android), but users reported suffering from lots of small irritations. A common issue was the bloatware that ships with the phone, overheating issues, keypad issues (pretty major for a phone that relies on a keypad).

**Lightphone**
The Light Phone company have, a the time of writing, released 3 phones. The latest phone is the most competent phone that realises the most of the company's vision of 'going light'. The phone is good and a stripped back experience and nice camera, with the ability to play music and podcasts that are downloaded to the phone from a computer. So far so good!

What turned me away from getting the Lightphone 3 was the lack of WhatsApp, and the price. For a puritan dumbphone connoisseur, this is very much the point of the phone. I'd give up all the other apps, like Uber, Strava and banking apps, but WhatsApp is something I didn't want to lose. I didn't want the dumphone aesthetic to supersede the function of a smartphone; I wanted to not be tempted by the addictive aspects of a smart phone. Also, the Lightphone 3 was still on the way to being released and came in batches of manufacturing. Its also $699, which, as an underpaid intern, was not something I wanted to splash on.

**Jelly Star**
Unihertz is a Chinese phone company that make some crazy phones. They've made an industrially tough smartphone with a projector built in [here](https://www.unihertz.com/products/tank-4-pro]), they've made an android 15 keyboard phone [here](https://www.unihertz.com/products/titan-2) and they make some very small phones. I saw the Jelly star and found it so appealing I was willing to give it a try. I bought the Unihertz Jelly Star for £180, and it did everything I needed in a form factor which limited the appeal of consuming content.

Sure, I *could* scroll reels on the thing - heck, I could play Genshin Impact on it thanks to its solid specs. But on such a tiny screen, anything beyond checking texts and taking photos becomes straining. Also, holding the phone is a constant reminder of my commitment to be less online.

For the sake of judging a phone by its specs, I won't discuss resolution or ram or its chipset - I will give my honest feedback on day to day usage. That's not to say the specs don't matter, but I only care about specs when it comes to the usability of the phone. If you're interested in the specs, go to the [website](https://www.unihertz.com/products/jelly-star)!

### How I've Set The Phone Up
Finally, we've reached the point in the story where I give you everything I've done to make my phone the best it can be.

**Homescreen**
For my homescreen, I use a custom android launcher. An android launcher replaces the normal homescreen of Android with something different, and they can be downloaded on the google play store. I'm using [Before Launcher](https://play.google.com/store/apps/details?id=com.beforesoft.launcher&hl=en_GB), but a couple of other popular ones I've seen are [Olauncher](https://play.google.com/store/apps/details?id=app.olauncher&hl=en_GB) and [Niagara Launcher](https://play.google.com/store/apps/details?id=bitpit.launcher&hl=en_GB).

![[Screenshot_20260617-220523.png]]

My background is a photo of the Birmingham skyline (where I live) with the northern lights above it! The interface is simple. Time, weather, battery percentage and my 4 most used apps.
Messages opens a sub-menu where I can choose to open Texts or WhatsApp

![[Screenshot_20260617-220746.png]]

Retro is a social media app that I'll talk more about later, but its a distant relative of BeReal, where a few friends and I post to our weeks. I hope the utility of the calendar and map apps are self explanatory!

When I swipe to the left, there is a notification centre. No notifications ever buzz my phone, with the exception of texts and calls. Everything else is filtered by Before Launcher and deposited in notifications grouped by apps in a nice minimal UI.

![[Screenshot_20260620-184208.png]]

If I swipe right, I see all the apps on my phone, as text entries in a scrollable menu, and put into groups. I can still access all my banking, Strava, Uber and any other apps like that, categorised into folders.

![[Screenshot_20260620-184227.png]]

**Buttons**
There are 4 buttons on the phone. Two connected volume buttons, a power button, and an assignable button (a pause / play / skip button by default). I've set up the assignable key as the following:
	1 press - Open Spotify
	2 presses - Open wallet (for NFC payments)
	Hold press - Turn on torch (phone screen doesn't need to be on for this to work)
I can open the camera by double pressing the power button. Speaking of the camera...

**Camera**
The stock camera app that comes with the phone is completely adequate for taking a photo. It even comes with a Pro mode to allow for manual control of the camera's parameters. But the post processing power really isn't there to create great photos, and the white balance isn't amazing.

The GCAM port for the Jelly Star allows me to use the photo processing present in pixel phones to make my photo's look much better! This gain comes at the cost of letting the photo process for 4 seconds before I can view it, which isn't great, but could also be seen as a way of slowing down the process. I found the GCAM porting information [here](https://www.reddit.com/r/unihertz/comments/1fp3xx8/release_decent_jelly_star_camera_app_config/) if you're interested in doing it too.

**Apps** - 
Here are some apps that I've started using since switching phones:

**Retro** - This is a social media app that let's user's post to their week, and see their friends weeks. A week is just a collection of photos, organised by day, and it has three weeks of retrospect, so there's only 4 pages of content in the whole app at any one point, which is much better than the infinite stream of mainstream social media apps - very anti-doomscrollable! The app let's me share photos with friends, and comment on their photos, and its just a nice experience, and a great trade away from Instagram. I'd recommend the app anyway, because its just good fun and its social media in its purest and best form. Here's a look at what this app looks like on the screen.

![[Screenshot_20260622-190812.png]]

**Voice Recorder** - It is worth giving a shout out to the voice recorder app by Fossify ([here](https://play.google.com/store/apps/details?id=org.fossify.voicerecorder&hl=en_GB)) which I've renamed voice recorder in the UI using Before Launcher. The stock voice recorder / memo app that comes with the phone is fine for taking voice notes, but it uses a voice isolation algorithm which completely destroys any musical audio. When I wanted to record an idea I'd just had on the guitar, I'd record it, then listen back to a garbled mess. The Fossify voice recorder app gives the option to bypass the processed recording pipeline and just give me the raw microphone signal (which is what I want). The only thing that could make this better would be normalising the recording when its stopped to make it louder, as most recordings come out quieter than I'd like.

**Other Features**

- It has an IR source on the top of the phone shell. Using an app that comes with the phone by default, I can control any TV I want, which can be entertaining. A strange feature on a phone, but a welcome one.
  
- There's a fingerprint sensor on the back of the phone to unlock the phone.
  
- There's an audio jack, so I can be hip and trendy and wear wired headphones and earphones.
  
- A small speaker on the bottom exists and its really not great in a technical sense, but it is good enough for listening to audiobooks or podcasts without headphones from a bedside table.
  
- I use night mode, which can be set to automatically turned on between overnight hours, and it sets the phone screen to black and white and lowers the brightness.


**Battery Life** - I found the battery life to be fine for my use. Its worth noting that the battery life of any phone is extended dramatically by using it less, and given I used the thing for about 45 minutes of screen-time a day, I hardly found myself running out of charge. It was very pleasant to go for three days without giving it a charge. Because of its very small battery, it also takes about 30 minutes for a full charge with a 20 Watt charger, meaning I could forget to charge it overnight and quickly charge it before heading out in the morning.

**Durability** - one of my party tricks when showing people my phone is to throw it on the floor over my shoulder to demonstrate how durable and brick like it is. I don't recommend this - whilst my phone has survived and works fine, I should probably stop doing this because it might break, but it hasn't yet. Hopefully that's testament enough to its general use durability.

# Results

So have I achieved my objectives? I wanted to be less addicted, and more social. Has this phone helped me do that?

In terms of measurable factors, here's a snapshot of a week of use, with the highest use measured. As you can see the average is around an hour a day, which is considerably better than the baseline 5 hours spent on a phone when using my iPhone 12. Not only that, but the most time spent on the example day in the screenshot is on WhatsApp, being social and planning things! This is how I want to use my phone - screen time should equal highly productive social planning and interacting.

![[Screenshot_20260617-231933.png]]

Also, note the lack of social media apps dominating my time. I no longer use Instagram and YouTube on a phone, and this has dramatically shifted my intake of online content. I'm also no longer tempted to install them, because I don't want to squint at the tiny display to watch content built for larger screens. I can still use the web versions of these apps on my laptop: I use a [browser extension](https://chromewebstore.google.com/detail/minimal-youtube/imnffafnaoooaedkhappfhomnnpigogk?hl=en&pli=1) to hide suggested content in YouTube, and I can use Instagram to message people from my laptop.

I can now go days without spending significant time on my phone - I can happily say that I'm not addicted to my phone anymore! I can also safely say that I have been more social. In the moments between things happening, instead of scrolling through my phone alone in my room, I get bored and want to hang out with friends instead. This must've been what it was like before the internet; getting bored meant finding something to do. It has reminded me of when I was a teenager - no phone, and boredom meant finding lots of things to do, and now I have a strangely broad set of very random skills.

Since changing phones, there have been a few side effects that I should report: I'm less anxious, and weeks feel longer. I've noticed I'm less anxious about the world, because I hear a lot less about it. I'm not an anxious person, and I wouldn't say I've ever had 'anxiety' in a mental health sense. But switching phones meant hearing less news, and less of the world, and less of other people's lives. I mostly see what's right in front of me, resulting in a sort of self sheltering which has done me loads of good. Turns out I really don't need to know about so much going on in the world - I haven't missed out on anything worthwhile!

I've also begun to experience time slightly differently. Days and weeks feel longer, and go by slower, and I suspect this is because I'm not erasing the 'time between'. The 'time between' consists of all the moments and minutes and hours between events in a day. A break between lectures; waiting for a meal to cook; gaps in the flow of the day. These are all moments that make up the 'time between' which was being completely snubbed out by doom-scrolling and spending time on my phone. Suddenly gaining this time back and needing to decide what to do with it had a massive shift on my sense of time in a day. This is a very welcome side effect!

# Ending thoughts

Now that I've compiled my ideas and phone setup on this page, I hope it can help you use your device more intentionally, or even consider making a switch to something new for a season. This week marks two weeks after finishing University, and for the first week back, I had a go at switching back to my iPhone 12.

After a year and a half of not using it, it felt huge, and colourful and fun. But very quickly I fell into old habits of spending hours watching reels, scrolling YouTube, and erasing the time-between. I also started feeling aching desires for things I hadn't felt until I picked my phone back up again. "I really need a girl" "I really need to get a house" "I just want to make something original" "I'm wasting my life" "I need to get into the gym 5 days a week". I suddenly made the connection - unwise use of social media really does affect me, and I know I'm not unique in that way.

I switched back after 5 days, and I'm happy to say I shall continue to use my tiny phone until it eventually breathes its last little digital breath.
